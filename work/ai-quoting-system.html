Business Problem

What was costing the business

Quotes took anywhere from ten to twenty minutes to prepare, and pricing varied depending on which surveyor wrote it. In a market where the fastest, most consistent quote often wins the job, that variability was a direct cost — both in lost deals to faster competitors and in margin risk from under- or over-quoting.

My Goal

What I set out to do

Deliberately not to invent a new pricing model. The business already had years of operational pricing knowledge — staff cost, fuel, vehicle profit margins, packing material pricing, van capacity rules — it just lived in individual surveyors' heads rather than in a system. My goal was to reverse-engineer that existing logic from real jobs and encode it faithfully, so every quote reflected the same commercial judgement regardless of who produced it.

Architecture

How it’s built

A client-side web app that accepts a docx survey upload, extracts room-by-room inventory using a local AI model, and runs it through a pricing engine built directly from real historical jobs — covering staff costs, fuel, vehicle profit, packing materials, and van capacity constraints. A companion Volume Calculator handles the inventory-to-cubic-footage step: it matches item descriptions against a catalogue of hundreds of standard furniture items using a deterministic rules engine first, and only calls an AI model for the handful of items the rules can't confidently match — keeping AI cost and latency reserved for genuine edge cases rather than every line item.

Implementation

The details that mattered

The rules-first, AI-fallback design in the Volume Calculator was a deliberate cost and speed decision: most inventory items match cleanly against the catalogue with rule-based matching, and only genuinely ambiguous or typo-heavy entries get sent to an AI model, with automatic fallback across models if one is rate-limited. The pricing logic itself came from studying real completed jobs rather than working from a theoretical formula, which meant the tool had to accommodate the actual messiness of real quotes, not an idealised version of them.

Business Outcome

What changed

Quote preparation reduced from approximately ten to twenty minutes to under one minute for a standard survey, while applying consistent commercial pricing logic regardless of who runs the quote. The salesperson still reviews and finalises every output — the tool removes the repetitive manual work, not the judgement.

Lessons Learned

What I took from it

Removing internal friction creates more selling time — the real win here wasn't a smarter pricing model, it was giving surveyors back the twenty minutes per quote they used to spend on arithmetic. I also learned that encoding existing expertise faithfully is often more valuable, and more defensible, than trying to redesign it from scratch.

Engineering Decisions

Why local AI (Ollama) for extraction?
Survey documents contain customer data. Running extraction locally means that data never has to leave the business.
Why rules first, AI as fallback?
Most inventory items match a known catalogue entry with a simple pattern. Reserving AI calls for genuinely ambiguous items keeps the tool fast and keeps AI cost proportional to actual difficulty, not total volume.
Why reverse-engineer the existing pricing model rather than design a new one?
The business already had years of correct commercial judgement built into how surveyors priced jobs. The risk wasn’t that the logic was wrong — it was that it only lived in people’s heads. Encoding it faithfully was more valuable, and more defensible, than replacing it.
